About this visualization

Design rationale

This visualization explores how California fire activity changed during wildfire season from 2000 to 2025. We used a map as the main view because fire detections are inherently spatial: plotting each thermal anomaly by latitude and longitude makes it easier to see where fire activity clusters across Southern California.

We considered grouping the data primarily by year, but we ultimately chose fire radiative power (FRP) as the main visual encoding. FRP acts as a proxy for fire intensity, so coloring points by FRP helps viewers distinguish weaker and stronger thermal anomalies directly on the map. The color scale uses darker colors for lower FRP values and brighter yellow-orange colors for stronger detections, matching the actual visual of fire in different temperatures.

The interaction techniques are designed to support focused exploration. The FRP range slider lets users filter by fire intensity, while the daytime/nighttime checkboxes allow comparison between detections made at different times of day. We capped the slider at 500+ MW because a few extreme FRP outliers would otherwise stretch the scale and make most detections difficult to filter meaningfully.

The vertical timeline shows fire detections over time and includes brush filtering, allowing users to select a specific date range and immediately update the map. We also added tooltips so users can inspect individual detections, including date, time, FRP, brightness, confidence, and coordinates.

Development process

We began by retrieving MODIS Terra thermal anomaly data from the NASA GIBS API and cleaning it to focus on California wildfire-season detections from August 1 to October 31. We then used geographic boundary tools to render the California map, added county outlines and major city labels for context, and plotted each fire detection using its latitude and longitude.

After building the base map, we added visual encodings for fire intensity, including the FRP color scale and legend. We then implemented the main interactions: FRP filtering, daytime/nighttime filtering, tooltip inspection, and timeline brush filtering. Finally, we refined the page layout and styling to make the visualization easier to read and interact with.

The work was divided across data collection and cleaning, map rendering, interaction design, chart implementation, and styling/debugging. With four team members, we estimate that the project took about 12 to 20 total people-hours.

The most time-consuming parts were getting the filters to update the map correctly and adjusting the SVG layout. Because the visualization combines multiple filters—FRP range, daytime/nighttime status, and brushed time range—we had to make sure they worked together consistently. The map layout also required extra adjustment because the original SVG canvas left too much empty space below the map.
